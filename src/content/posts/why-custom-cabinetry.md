If you are comparing custom cabinetry with a flat-pack or showroom alternative, the obvious difference is the price tag. The less obvious difference — and the one that matters most — is what happens five years after installation.

## Fit

Off-the-shelf cabinets are designed to fit standard spaces. If your walls are square, your floors are level, and your room dimensions match a manufacturer's module grid, they work well enough. Most Australian homes do not meet those conditions. Custom cabinetry is built to the actual room — not to a theoretical one.

## Materials

Flat-pack cabinets use particleboard with a paper-thin melamine layer. They are fine for rental properties and short-term use. Custom cabinetry uses plywood or moisture-resistant MDF for carcases, solid timber or engineered wood for doors, and Blum or Hettich hardware throughout. The difference in feel and durability is immediate.

## Longevity

A well-built custom kitchen will outlast two flat-pack kitchens. The hardware will still run smoothly, the doors will still align, and the finish will not peel at the edges. For most homeowners, that makes the custom option cheaper over time.

## Resale

Custom cabinetry is a selling point. Real estate agents know it, buyers notice it, and the investment typically returns more than it cost when the property is sold.

Custom cabinetry is not the budget option. It is the best-value option for people who plan to stay in their home and care about how it functions.
